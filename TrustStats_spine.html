Trusts Statistics

September 2019 National Statistics

2013-14 to 2017-18

  • In 2017-18, trusts and estates had a total income of £2.73 billion which was an increase of 12% on the previous year. The total amount of chargeable gains was £3.23 billion, a negligible change to the previous year.

  • The number of trusts and estates completing Self Assessment forms has continued to fall. In 2017-18, there were 149,000 trusts and estates which is a fall of 6% from 159,000 in 2016-17. Of these, 58% are trusts paying tax at the special trust rates.

  • The total tax bill in 2017-18 for trusts and estates was £1.32 billion. Of this, 51% was income tax and the rest capital gains.

1 Commentary

1.1 Summary of key statistics

  • Latest data shows that 49,000 interest in possession trusts made selfassessment returns for tax year 2017-18, around 4,000 fewer than in 2016-17.

  • 86,500 trusts paying tax at the trusts rate made Self Assessment returns for tax year 2017-18, a fall of 4,000.

  • Income of interest in possession trusts and trusts paying Income Tax at the special trust rates in 2017-18 totalled £2,470 million, 12% up on the previous year.

  • Total Income Tax payable on income of trusts and estates in 2017-18 was £675 million. Of this, £140 million arose from interest in possession trusts and £495 million from trusts taxable at the special trust rates. The remainder arose from “Other” trusts, such as charities, and non-trust structures, namely estates.

  • For trusts paying tax at the trust rates, chargeable gains in 2017-18 were £1,445 million, representing a 3% fall on 2016-17; chargeable gains for interest in possession trusts were £1,455 million in 2017-18, approximately the same as in 2016-17.

  • Capital Gains Tax payable on gains made by trusts and estates was £645 million in 2017-18, up 1% on 2016-17.

  • The Trust Registration Service had 107,500 registrations as of 5th MArch 2019, an increase of 22,500 on the previous year.

1.2 Overall Trends

Figure 1: Total number of trusts and estates

Figure 1 shows that the total number of trusts and estates fell in 2017-18 representing a fourth year of consecutive declines. This continues the long term downward trend. In 2005-06 there were around 220,500 trusts and estates in the UK, which has fallen to around 149,500 in 2017-18. This corresponds to a drop of 29% over the past 13 years.

Figure 2: Number of trusts, and income of trusts paying tax at the special trust rates by band of income in 2016-17

Figure 2 shows the number and income of trusts paying tax at the special trust rates. There were around 86,500 trusts paying Income Tax at the special trust rates in 2017-18. 40% had income of less than £1,000 which is similar to that observed in previous years. Only 3,000 trusts had income of more than £100,000 in 2017-18.

The total income of trusts paying Income Tax at the special trust rates was £1,385 million in 2017-18, up by around 14% compared with 2016-17. Trusts with income of more than £100,000 accounted for around 59% of the total income in 201718 compared with 53% in 2016-17 and 63% in 2014-15.

Figure 3: Number of interest in possession trusts and income of these trusts by income band in 2016-17

There were 49,000 interest in possession trusts in 2017-18. Figure 3 shows the distribution of these trusts by band of income. Around 41% of interest in possession trusts had income of less than £1,000, whilst around 3,000 trusts had income of more than £100,000

Figure 4: Income of interest in possession trusts by type of income

Figure 4 shows the level of income from interest in possession trusts by type of income. Total income had behaved erratically over the past five years. The total growth rate across the five years from 2013-14 to 2017-18 for total income for trusts and estates was 12%.

Figure 5 shows the level of income for trusts paying Income Tax at the special rates. In 2017-18 the total income of trusts taxable at the special trust rates by type of income rose by around 14% from the previous year. This increase partially recovers after a sharp fall in 2016-17.

This change is mostly driven by changes in levels of dividend income.

Figure 5: Tax payable on income of trusts

Figure 6 shows tax payable on income of trusts, split between different types of trust. Total Income Tax rose to £675m in 2017-18, a gain of 9% compared with 2016-17. Income from interest in possession trusts and trusts paying tax at the trusts rate declined by 8% and 10% respectively over the same period.

Figure 6: Number of interest in possession trusts by income band

Figure 6 shows the number of trusts in each income band. The buttons allow comparison of interest in possession trusts and trusts paying tax at the special trust rates.

Regarding interest in possession trusts, previously the majority of these trusts had income of less than £1,000, but this has changed in recent years. The number of trusts with income over £100,000 has increased year on year since 2013-14 rising to around 3,000 in 2017-18.

In general, the number of interest in possession trusts continues to follow a downward trend.

The number of trusts paying Income Tax at the special trust rates by income band in 2016-17 saw a 5% decline in the number of trusts with under £10,000 of income from the previous year and a 14% fall over a five year timeframe.

Trusts with total income over $10,000 have seen a fall of 4% in the past year going against the recent trend which still shows an 8% increase over the past 5 years.

Annex A: Data sources, methodology and data quality

Data sources

Statistics are compiled using information on self-assessment tax returns. This information is contained on HMRC’s departmental administrative system CESA (Computerised Environment for Self-Assessment). This is an administrative data source; no sampling of data is required in order to produce these statistics.

A data mart is created each month containing all information on CESA at that time. The data mart created at the end of December 2018 has been used to produce these statistics.

The date for submitting self-assessment returns to HMRC depends on the way in which returns are sent. Returns completed on paper are due for return on 31 October following the end of the relevant tax year, and returns completed online are due on 31 January following the end of the tax year. Information from the tax return is not included within the trusts statistics until the year after returns are due. This means that the bulk of tax returns are available for all years for which statistics are published. However, returns continue to be received or updated after the return due dates so figures in all years are liable to change when statistics are updated.

Trusts that do not require completion of a self-assessment return are not captured within these statistics.

Methodology

The trust statistics are compiled using aggregated information collected in the trust and estates pages of the self-assessment return. The 2016-17 return is available on line here:

https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/self-assessment-trust-and-estate-tax-return-sa900

The count of trusts is based on the number of completed tax returns on the CESA data mart.

Income figures are collated from information supplied on tax returns.

The tax calculation is applied to information on tax returns and tax figures are derived by selecting stages from the calculation. The 2016-17 tax calculation guide is available online here:

https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/self-assessment-trust-and-estate-tax-return-sa900

The tax return and tax calculation will vary from year to year.

Data quality

The quality of these statistics is assessed against the six European Statistical Service dimensions of quality developed by Eurostat to ensure they are appropriate for publication. A summary of adherence with each dimension is published here:

https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/quality-report-trusts
